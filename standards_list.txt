2.1.4  Customer Needs 
The customer needs identified for this project are as follows: 
1. The system must detect methane leaks reliably under controlled conditions. 
2. The system must support safe laboratory testing without requiring field deployment. 
3. The system must provide clear and interpretable leak indication to the user. 
4. The system must reduce false alarms caused by noise or transient effects. 
5. The system must support future scalability and adaptation to more advanced 
monitoring solutions. 
 
2.1.5  Standards 
The design and operation of the proposed methane leak detection system follow a 
limited set of safety, laboratory, communication, and data security standards that are directly 
relevant to the project constraints and specifications. These standards are cons idered to ensure 
safe laboratory testing, reliable system operation, and secure data transmission:

• OSHA (Occupational Safety and Health Administration) standards related to the 
handling of flammable gases and safe operation of electrical equipment during 
laboratory testing. 
• Lower Explosive Limit (LEL) safety guidelines for methane, used to define safe 
detection thresholds below hazardous concentration levels. 
• Advanced Encryption Standard (AES-128) for securing transmitted sensor data. 
• KFUPM laboratory safety regulations governing calibration of gases, experimental 
procedures, and laboratory operation. 
 
2.1.6  Constraints 
The proposed methane leak detection system is subject to the following constraints, 
which define the boundaries and limitations within which the design, implementation, and 
testing must be conducted: 
1. The total cost of the prototype shall not exceed 15,000 SAR. 
2. System sensitivity shall be limited to ppm -level methane detection, prioritizing low 
power consumption and system simplicity over ppb-level sensitivity. 
3. Methane measurement accuracy may be affected by environmental factors, including 
humidity, carbon dioxide (CO₂), and other hydrocarbons. 
4. Optical alignment between the infrared source and detector must be maintained to 
ensure reliable methane detection. 
5. The system shall operate under outdoor pipeline conditions, including 0 –60 °C 
temperature, up to 95% relative humidity (non-condensing), and dust exposure. 
6. Calibration of gases and testing procedures must comply with KFUPM laboratory 
requirements. 
7. System operation must comply with OSHA safety standards, particularly those related 
to flammable gas handling and laboratory electrical safety. 
8. Infrared source of intensity drifts, and ambient infrared noise may affect long -term 
signal stability.
