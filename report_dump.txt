Abstract 
Methane leakage from pipeline systems poses significant safety, environmental, and economic 
risks, requiring reliable and timely detection methods. This project presents the design and 
implementation of a bench-top methane leak detection system based on infrared absorption principles. 
Infrared light is transmitted across controlled leak regions of a pipeline section, where the presence of 
methane is detected through attenuation of specific wavelengths caused by gas absorption. 
A laboratory pipeline test rig with controlled methane leakage points was constructed to simulate 
realistic leak scenarios. An infrared emitter–detector pair was installed at selected locations along the 
pipeline to monitor variations in received signal intensity. The detected analog signal is conditioned 
and digitized using a microcontroller, then transmitted to a cloud platform via a cellular communication 
module for remote monitoring. 
Artificial intelligence techniques are implemented to analyze the infrared sensor data and distinguish 
between normal operating conditions and methane leakage events. Machine learning models are trained 
using labeled experimental data to classify leak presence and estimate leak severity based on signal 
patterns and temporal variations. A software application retrieves data from the cloud and provides real-
time leak alerts, signal visualization, and event logging. 
System performance is evaluated through controlled experiments by varying leak rates and operating 
conditions. Key performance metrics include detection accuracy, response time, false alarm rate, and 
communication reliability. The results demonstrate the feasibility of combining infrared sensing, 
cellular communication, and AI-based analysis for effective methane leak detection in pipeline systems.

Chapter 1  
Background 
1.1 Overview 
Methane leakage from pipeline systems represents a persistent challenge in terms of 
safety, environmental impact, and operational efficiency. Pipelines often extend across long 
distances and operate in locations that are difficult to continuously monitor, making early leak 
detection a complex task. Undetected methane leaks can escalate into hazardous situations, 
contribute to greenhouse gas emissions, and result in increased operational and maintenance 
costs. 
Current methane detection practices rely heavily on fixed sensing units or periodic inspections, 
which may provide limited coverage and delayed response. These approaches can struggle to 
detect small or intermittent leaks and are often affected by environmental conditions and sensor 
noise. As a result, there is a need for alternative detection approaches that allow controlled 
evaluation of sensing performance and system behavior. 
This design project addresses these challenges by offering a bench-top methane leak detection 
system that enables controlled laboratory testing of methane sensing concepts. By focusing on 
a laboratory-scale prototype, the project provides a safe, repeatable, and cost-effective platform 
to study methane detection using infrared absorption principles and integrated system 
components. 
1.2 Introduction 
Methane is widely used and transported in oil and gas infrastructure, making reliable 
leak detection a critical requirement for safe operation. Even minor methane leaks can pose 
significant safety risks due to the flammable nature of the gas and can contribute to 
environmental harm through increased greenhouse gas emissions. The difficulty of 
continuously monitoring extensive pipeline networks highlights the need for detection systems 
that can identify leaks early and reliably. 

--- PAGE 11 ---
 
 
 
12 
The motivation for this project arises from the limitations of existing methane detection 
approaches, particularly in scenarios where controlled testing and system validation are 
required. Many commercial detection systems are designed for fixed installations or field 
deployment and are often costly, complex, and difficult to adapt for experimental evaluation. 
As a result, there is a gap between advanced industrial solutions and laboratory-scale systems 
that allow systematic investigation of methane sensing performance. 
The oil and gas industry is the primary sector affected by methane leakage and stands to benefit 
from improved detection technologies. Pipeline operators, inspection of service providers, and 
safety and regulatory bodies all require dependable methods to identify leaks and assess system 
integrity. In recent years, the industry has increasingly adopted data-driven and intelligent 
monitoring approaches to enhance detection of reliability, reduce false alarms, and support 
operational decision-making. These approaches commonly combine sensing hardware with 
software-based data analysis, rather than relying solely on fixed threshold alarms. 
Several methane detection solutions are currently available, including fixed gas detectors, 
portable handheld sensors, and advanced optical systems such as laser-based and remote 
sensing technologies. Fixed detectors provide continuous monitoring at specific locations but 
offer limited coverage along extended pipeline segments. Handheld sensors require manual 
inspection and may fail to detect transient or low-level leaks consistently. While advanced 
optical systems offer high sensitivity, they are typically expensive and impractical for 
laboratory-scale experimentation or early-stage concept validation. In addition, many existing 
systems rely on simple threshold-based triggering, which can be sensitive to noise and 
environmental variability. 
To address these limitations, the proposed project integrates artificial intelligence as a 
supportive analysis layer within a bench-top methane detection system. Rather than replacing 
the physical sensing mechanism, AI techniques are used to analyze infrared sensor data patterns 
and assist in distinguishing normal operating conditions from methane leakage events. This 
data-driven approach aims to improve detection reliability and reduce false alarms caused by 
transient disturbances or sensor noise. By combining infrared sensing, embedded processing, 
cloud-based data handling, and AI-assisted analysis, the project aligns with current industry 
trends toward intelligent monitoring systems while maintaining a controlled and experimentally 
verifiable laboratory scope.

1.3 Fundamental Scientific and Engineering Concepts 
This project is based on a combination of fundamental scientific principles and core 
engineering concepts drawn from petroleum engineering, electrical engineering, and computer 
science. These concepts collectively enable the detection of methane leaks, the conversion of 
physical phenomena into measurable electrical signals, and the analysis of sensor data within a 
controlled laboratory environment. 
1.3.1  Infrared Absorption Principle for Methane Detection 
The fundamental scientific principle underlying the proposed methane detection 
system is infrared (IR) absorption. Methane molecules absorb infrared radiation at specific 
wavelengths due to molecular vibrational modes. When infrared light propagates through a 
region containing methane gas, a portion of the incident radiation is absorbed, resulting in a 
reduction in the transmitted light intensity. 
This absorption behavior is described by the Beer–Lambert principle, which relates the amount 
of absorbed radiation to the gas concentration and the optical path length. The conceptual 
behavior of this relationship is illustrated in Figure 1, where infrared radiation emitted from a 
light source pass through a gas-filled optical path of length ￼L, and the transmitted light 
intensity decreases due to absorption by methane molecules. The mathematical representation 
and parameter definitions of this relationship are shown in Figure 2, where the absorbance 
depends on the molar absorption coefficient, gas concentration, and optical path length.

In the context of this project, the Beer–Lambert principle is not used to calculate precise 
methane concentration values. Instead, it provides a physical basis for understanding how the 
presence of methane causes measurable attenuation of infrared radiation. The system relies on 
detecting relative changes in transmitted signal intensity, which is sufficient for identifying leak 
events under controlled laboratory conditions. 
From a petroleum engineering perspective, this principle supports the analysis of methane 
behavior in pipeline leak scenarios, where gas presence within the optical path indicates

leakage. Controlled leak simulation allows systematic evaluation of how methane accumulation 
affects optical transmission and detection reliability. 
 
1.3.2  Infrared Sensing Configuration 
To implement the infrared absorption principle, the system uses an infrared emitter–
detector pair positioned across selected regions of the pipeline test section. The emitter 
generates continuous infrared radiation directed toward the detector. Under normal operating 
conditions, the detector receives a stable signal corresponding to unobstructed transmission. 
When methane leaks into the optical path, partial absorption of infrared radiation occurs, 
leading to a reduction in the detected signal intensity. This configuration represents a non-
dispersive infrared sensing approach, which is suitable for bench-top experimentation due to 
its simplicity, robustness, and repeatability. Mechanical alignment, optical shielding, and fixed 
geometry are used to minimize the influence of ambient light and environmental disturbances. 
 
1.3.3  Signal Conditioning and Data Acquisition 
From an electrical engineering standpoint, the infrared detector produces a low-
amplitude analog electrical signal that is susceptible to noise and interference. To ensure 
reliable measurement, signal conditioning is applied before digital processing. This includes 
amplification to increase signal magnitude and filtering to suppress unwanted noise 
components. 
The conditioned signal is then digitized using an analog-to-digital converter (ADC) integrated 
within a microcontroller. Digitization enables consistent sampling, numerical representation of 
the sensor output, and time-stamping of measurements. These steps are essential for reliable 
data transmission and subsequent software-based analysis. 
In the final system design, digitized measurements are structured into time-stamped data frames 
and transmitted securely to the backend using HTTPS, ensuring encrypted communication 
between the embedded system and cloud infrastructure. 

--- PAGE 15 ---
 
 
 
16 
 The microcontroller functions as the central embedded system, managing sensor sampling, 
local data buffering for time-series continuity, and secure communication with external 
platforms. 
 
 
1.3.4  Data Transmission and Cloud-Based Monitoring 
 Communication engineering concepts are applied through secure HTTPS-based data 
transmission from the embedded system to the backend cloud platform.  All transmitted data is 
secured using Transport Layer Security (TLS), which performs authenticated key exchange and 
encrypted communication using AES-based symmetric encryption after session establishment. 
This architecture reflects modern monitoring system designs used in industrial and research 
applications. 
 The FastAPI backend and cloud database serves as an intermediate layer that allows sensor 
data to be accessed by higher-level software applications without direct physical interaction 
with the hardware. 
 
1.3.5  Data-Driven Analysis and Artificial Intelligence 
From a computer science perspective, the project employs data-driven analysis 
techniques to support methane leak detection. Rather than relying solely on fixed threshold 
values, sensor data is analyzed to identify patterns associated with normal operation and leak 
events. Features such as signal magnitude variation and temporal behavior are extracted from 
the infrared measurements. 
 An unsupervised anomaly detection approach based on the Isolation Forest algorithm is 
applied to model normal operating behavior and detect deviations indicative of methane 
leakage. Instead of relying on labeled leak data, the model learns baseline signal patterns and 
assigns an anomaly score to new observations based on structural deviation in the feature space. 
The role of artificial intelligence in this project is  to provide predictive anomaly scoring and

graded decision support, complementing the physical sensing mechanism rather than replacing 
it. 
 
 
1.3.6  System-Level Integration 
The integration of infrared sensing, signal conditioning, embedded processing, wireless 
communication, and data-driven analysis forms a complete methane leak detection system. 
Each subsystem performs a specific function, and overall performance depends on their 
coordinated operation. Real-time inference within the backend ensures that anomaly detection 
results are generated within the defined system response constraint, while secure transmission 
and structured data handling maintain reliability across subsystem boundaries.   From a systems 
engineering perspective, the project emphasizes integration and experimental validation rather 
than component-level optimization. 
By combining petroleum engineering context, electrical sensing hardware, and computer 
science–based data analysis, the project provides a multidisciplinary platform for evaluating 
methane leak detection behavior, response time, and reliability under controlled laboratory 
conditions. 
 
1.3.7  Summary 
The application of infrared absorption physics, basic signal processing, embedded 
system design, wireless communication, and data-driven predictive anomaly detection forms 
the scientific and engineering foundation of the proposed methane detection system. These 
concepts collectively enable the implementation and evaluation of a bench-top prototype that 
supports controlled testing, performance assessment, and future scalability while remaining 
aligned with academic design requirements and project scope.

3. Methane measurement accuracy may be affected by environmental factors, including 
humidity, carbon dioxide (CO₂), and other hydrocarbons. 
4. Optical alignment between the infrared source and detector must be maintained to 
ensure reliable methane detection. 
5. The system shall operate under outdoor pipeline conditions, including 0–60 °C 
temperature, up to 95% relative humidity (non-condensing), and dust exposure. 
6. Calibration of gases and testing procedures must comply with KFUPM laboratory 
requirements. 
7. System operation must comply with OSHA safety standards, particularly those related 
to flammable gas handling and laboratory electrical safety. 
8. Infrared source of intensity drifts, and ambient infrared noise may affect long-term 
signal stability.

2.1.7  Specifications 
The proposed methane leak detection system shall satisfy the following technical and 
functional specifications. These specifications are stated as measurable and verifiable 
performance requirements and are directly derived from the project constraints and objectives: 
1. The system shall detect methane (CH₄) only as the target gas. 
2. Methane detection shall be based on open-path infrared absorption using a fixed 
emitter–detector (NDIR / opto-pair) configuration. 
3. The infrared source shall operate at methane absorption wavelengths within 1.63–1.69 
µm (near-infrared) or 3.2–3.4 µm (mid-infrared). 
4. The optical path length between the infrared emitter and detector shall be fixed and 
known during operation. 
5. The methane detection threshold shall be set below 1% of the Lower Explosive Limit 
(LEL). 
6. Local methane leak detection at the microcontroller level shall occur within ≤ 2 seconds 
of sensing. 
7. End-to-end alert delivery to the user application shall occur within ≤ 10 seconds. 
8. The infrared signal shall be sampled at a rate of ≥ 10 Hz. 
9. Narrowband optical filtering shall be used to isolate methane-sensitive wavelengths 
with a defined passband matching the selected absorption range. 
10. AI-based leak classification shall achieve a minimum accuracy of ≥ 70% in 
distinguishing true leak events from noise. 
11. Methane monitoring shall be performed at fixed sensing locations along the pipeline. 
12. Sensor data shall be transmitted to the cloud using cellular communication.

methane detection under laboratory conditions. The second deliverable is a time-series methane

no-leak, slow-leak, and sudden-leak conditions, are simulated in a safe and controlled manner. 
The infrared sensor records methane concentration at fixed time intervals, producing structured 
datasets with timestamps. These datasets serve as the foundation for subsequent data analysis

conventional safety thresholds, ensuring that system reliability and safety are preserved. 
Performance metrics such as detection accuracy and response time are used to assess the 
effectiveness of this deliverable. The fourth deliverable is a real-time monitoring and alert 
output, which presents sensor readings and AI-based decisions to the user. This includes live 
visualization of methane concentration, system status indicators, and warning notifications 
when abnormal behavior is detected. The output demonstrates how the proposed system can 
support operators in identifying potential leaks earlier and more reliably than traditional fixed-
threshold systems. Collectively, these deliverables demonstrate a complete and integrated 
system that combines infrared methane sensing with intelligent data interpretation, fulfilling 
the project objectives.

This section documents the process used to narrow down the potential design concepts 
and select the final solution. A Design Concept Fan, shown in Figure 5, was used as a structured

concept fan, circles represent decision points, while rectangles represent specific solution

Concept Fan Structure and Decision Flow 
At the highest level, the design was decomposed into major subsystems, including the 
sensing method, infrared source configuration, receiver type, signal processing approach, 
communication method, and user interface. Each subsystem was treated as a decision node, 
from which multiple solution options were explored. 
 
Sensing Method Selection 
One of the primary decision points involved selecting the methane sensing method. 
The alternatives considered included contact-based gas sensors, air-sampling sensors, and 
optical infrared sensing. Contact-based sensors were rejected due to sensor poisoning, drift, and 
frequent calibration requirements. Air-sampling systems were found to have slower response 

--- PAGE 39 ---
 
 
 
40 
times and limited spatial coverage. Infrared optical absorption sensing was selected due to its 
non-contact operation, high selectivity for methane, and suitability for continuous monitoring. 
 
Infrared Source Configuration 
For the infrared source, options included a single emitter, a linear emitter arrangement, 
and a circumferential source configuration. A single emitter offered simplicity but limited 
coverage. The infrared source ring was selected as it provides uniform illumination around the 
pipeline and improves the probability of detecting leaks occurring at different circumferential 
locations. 
 
Receiver Type Selection 
Receiver options included thermal detectors, phototransistors, and photodiode-based 
receivers. Thermal detectors were rejected due to slower response times, while phototransistors 
were found to be more sensitive to noise. Photodiode-based receivers, matched to methane 
absorption wavelengths, were selected due to their fast response, stability, and compatibility 
with precise signal conditioning. 
 
Signal Processing and Decision Making 
Signal processing alternatives included simple threshold-based detection and machine 
learning-based classification. While threshold-based methods are easy to implement, they are 
highly susceptible to false alarms caused by environmental noise. AI-based signal classification 
was selected to improve detection reliability and reduce false positives by learning 
characteristic absorption patterns associated with methane leakage. 
 
Communication and User Interface 
Communication options included wired transmission, short-range wireless 
communication, and cellular communication. Cellular communication was selected due to its 
suitability for remote pipeline environments. For user interaction, local displays were 
considered but ultimately replaced with a mobile/desktop application, providing remote alerts, 
visualization, and event logging. 
 
Comparison of Alternative Concepts and Trade-Offs 
Several complete system concepts were formed by combining different branches of the 
concept fan, including a mobile robotic inspection system, distributed fixed sensors, fixed 

--- PAGE 40 ---
 
 
 
41 
sensors with air sampling, and an infrared-based optical detection system. Mobile robotic 
systems offered mobility but introduced mechanical complexity, higher power consumption, 
and safety concerns. Distributed fixed sensors reduced mechanical complexity but suffered 
from limited coverage. Air-sampling systems improved sensitivity but had slower response 
times. 
The infrared-based open-path methane leak detection system provides the best balance between 
safety, coverage, reliability, power efficiency, and feasibility. Its non-intrusive design 
eliminates the need for mechanical movement, while circumferential infrared coverage and 
intelligent data processing enhance leak detection performance. 
 
 
 
Final Concept Selection 
Based on the concept fan analysis and qualitative comparison of alternatives, the 
infrared-based methane leak detection system was selected as the final design. The selected 
configuration consists of an infrared source ring mounted externally at one end of the pipeline, 
and a photodiode-based receiver mounted externally at the opposite end. Methane leakage is 
detected through attenuation of infrared light along the pipeline, and the resulting signals are 
processed using AI techniques and transmitted to a user's application via cellular 
communication. 
The final concept selected through this process is further evaluated quantitatively using a Pugh 
matrix, which is presented in Section 3.3.

intensity. This absorption is observable in the infrared spectrum and is used to identify the 
presence of methane leakage.

4.1 Overall Description  
The final prototype is designed to detect and monitor methane leakage at critical 
pipeline joint locations, including flanges, gaskets, and valve connections. These locations are 
widely recognized as the most vulnerable points in gas transmission infrastructure due to 
mechanical stress, seal degradation, corrosion, and repeated pressure and temperature 
variations over time [4.1], [12], [13].  
The final design of the proposed methane leak detection system is a fixed, free-space laser-
based sensing system integrated with embedded signal processing, AI-based classification, and 
cloud monitoring. 
The system consists of a near-infrared laser diode transmitter mounted at one end of the pipeline 
test section, and a photodetector mounted at the opposite end. The laser beam propagates 
directly through air between the two ends. When methane leaks into the region between the 
transmitter and receiver, the gas absorbs part of the infrared radiation at a methane-sensitive 
wavelength. This absorption reduces the received infrared signal intensity at the detector. 
The photodetector converts the received infrared radiation into an electrical current. This 
current is converted into a voltage signal using a transimpedance amplifier and further 

--- PAGE 48 ---
 
 
 
49 
conditioned through analog filtering to reduce noise. The conditioned signal is then digitized 
using a microcontroller with an integrated analog-to-digital converter (ADC).  
The digital data is processed and analyzed using an AI-based classification algorithm to 
determine whether a methane leak is present. The processed signal is then analyzed through a 
supervised machine learning algorithm (Random Forest classifier) that has been trained on 
labeled methane and non-methane signal patterns. The algorithm assesses the extracted 
features, including the level of signal attenuation and the rate of signal change, to effectively 
distinguish between real methane leaks and natural environmental variations. 
The detection results are transmitted to a cloud platform using a cellular communication 
module. A user application retrieves the data from the cloud and provides real-time monitoring, 
signal visualization, and leak alerts. 
Detection thresholds are set using conservative limits referenced to the Lower Explosive Limit 
(LEL), so that alerts are based on meaningful safety conditions. This approach helps translate 
infrared measurements into practical pipeline safety decisions while keeping the sensing 
hardware, data analysis, and safety interpretation aligned.

Baseline calibration is performed to correct slow drift effects caused by temperature

interfere with methane detection.

Rather than relying on individual samples, a sliding time-window approach is implemented to

over time, which is essential for predictive leak detection. Within each window, dynamic 
features such as rate of change, rolling average, and signal stability are extracted. These features 
capture gradual trends, subtle drift patterns, and increasing variability that may indicate early 
methane presence. 
By representing the signal as a structured set of temporal features rather than raw intensity 
values, the CS subsystem provides the predictive AI model with meaningful inputs for anomaly 
scoring. This feature engineering strategy improves detection robustness, enhances early 
warning capability, and reduces false positives caused by transient noise or isolated 
fluctuations. 
 
4.2.3.3 AI Classification and Decision Logic 
The processed and feature-engineered signal is analyzed using an unsupervised anomaly 
detection model based on the Isolation Forest algorithm. Unlike supervised classification 
approaches that require labeled leak and non-leak data, Isolation Forest is trained primarily on 
normal operating behavior and identifies deviations from that baseline as anomalies. 
This design choice is motivated by the nature of methane leak detection in pipeline 
environments, where leak events are rare and early-stage leak signatures may not follow 
consistent labeled patterns. By modeling normal system behavior, the AI subsystem can detect 
subtle deviations in absorbance trends, signal variability, and temporal dynamics that precede 
confirmed leak conditions. 
The Isolation Forest model operates by recursively partitioning the multidimensional feature 
space. Data points that require fewer partitions to isolate are assigned higher anomaly scores, 
indicating abnormal behavior. Because the algorithm evaluates structural deviation rather than 
fixed thresholds, it is well-suited for predictive detection in dynamic operating conditions. 
Instead of producing a simple binary output, the anomaly score is mapped into three operational 
states:

 • Early Warning (Yellow) – Moderate anomaly score indicating emerging deviation trends.

The model is deployed within the FastAPI backend, where inference is performed in real time

By combining time-series feature analysis with anomaly scoring and graded alert thresholds,

design objectives.

4.3.2.1 Analytical Framework for Methane Leak Detectability 
The petroleum-engineering role of the final design focuses on verifying that the 
infrared sensing configuration can reliably identify methane leakage under physically realistic 
dispersion conditions while maintaining strict compliance with flammability-safety constraints. 
The design follows OSHA LEL standards of safety. Rather than evaluating methane presence 
only as a sensor signal variation, the analysis interprets detection in terms of loss of 
containment, dispersion behavior, and proximity to explosion limits, which represent the 
governing engineering risks in gas-pipeline systems. 
Methane concentration is therefore referenced to the Lower Explosive Limit (LEL) to provide 
a direct safety-oriented metric. For methane: 
100% LEL CH4 ≈ 5% vol ≈ 50,000 ppm 
To normalize measurements relative to flammability risk, concentration is expressed as: 
% LEL= (Gas concentration (vol) / 5%) *100 
This formulation establishes a quantitative relationship between measured methane 
concentration, ignition hazard, and operational decision thresholds, enabling engineering-level 
interpretation of detection results. 

--- PAGE 70 ---
 
 
 
71 
4.3.2.1 Safety Margin and Early-Warning Detection Philosophy 
A key design objective of the final system is operation within the sub-LEL regime, 
ensuring that methane detection occurs significantly before concentrations approach flammable 
conditions. This transforms the sensing system from a reactive hazard indicator into 
a preventive early-warning mechanism, consistent with petroleum-industry risk-management 
philosophy where mitigation is prioritized prior to escalation. 
Let Cd denote the minimum detectable methane concentration expressed in %LEL. 
Safe early-warning operation requires: 
Cd ≪ 100% LEL  
and practically: 
Cd ≤ 1–5% LEL  
Operating within this conservative range provides: 
• 
Large safety margin from ignition risk 
• 
Sufficient time for corrective intervention 
• 
Reduced probability of fire or explosion escalation 
Thus, the LEL-referenced framework defines both technical detectability and safety 
compliance simultaneously. 
4.3.2.2 Methane Dispersion Physics and Distance-Dependent 
Detectability 
Following leakage at a pipeline joint, methane forms a localized turbulent plume whose 
concentration decreases with radial distance due to atmospheric dilution and mixing. Although 
full computational fluid-dynamics modeling is outside project scope, dispersion behavior can 
be represented using a simplified power-law decay: 
C(x) ∝ (1/x^n), n > 0 
where: 

--- PAGE 71 ---
 
 
 
72 
• 
x = distance from leak source 
• 
n = empirical dispersion exponent influenced by turbulence and airflow 
This relationship provides several engineering implications: 
1. Detectability is strongly distance-dependent, requiring sensor placement near probable 
leak sources. 
2. Joint-level monitoring is justified, since methane concentration is highest close to the 
containment failure. 
3. Validation testing must include multiple distances to confirm consistent detection 
within the expected near-field region. 
These conclusions reinforce the petroleum-engineering decision to focus sensing at pipeline 
joints rather than along intact pipe segments.

envelope bounded by:

4.5  Cost Analysis  
The total estimated cost of the final methane leak detection prototype is 14,769 SAR, which 
satisfies the project constraint of maintaining the total prototype cost below 15,000 SAR. The 

--- PAGE 84 ---
 
 
 
85 
cost distribution reflects a deliberate balance between sensing performance, safety 
considerations, and financial feasibility at the laboratory prototype stage.  
A significant portion of the budget is allocated to the optical subsystem, including the 1650 nm 
laser diode, InGaAs photodetector, laser mount, and current controller. These components 
represent the core detection mechanism of the system. Since methane detection relies directly 
on infrared absorption and precise optical stability, investing in reliable optical hardware was 
necessary to ensure measurement accuracy, signal consistency, and safe laser operation. The 
laser mount and current controller, although relatively high in cost, provide temperature 
stability and controlled excitation, which are essential for maintaining wavelength integrity and 
avoiding signal drift.  
Mechanical components, such as pipeline sections and joints, account for a smaller but essential 
portion of the total cost. These elements enable realistic simulation of joint-level methane 
leakage under controlled laboratory conditions. Their inclusion ensures that experimental valid 
ation reflects practical containment-loss scenarios rather than abstract gas exposure testing. 
The software-related costs, including application deployment and cloud hosting, represent a 
relatively minor fraction of the total budget. However, they significantly enhance the system’s 
functionality. The cloud platform enables remote monitoring and structured data storage, while 
the mobile application provides real-time alerts and visualization. The AI-based classification 
layer further improves reliability by reducing false alarms and enhancing decision support. 
Notably, these software components increase system capability without substantially increasing 
overall cost.  
Overall, the cost structure demonstrates that a technically robust, multidisciplinary methane 
detection prototype can be implemented within the defined academic budget constraints. The 
final cost remains below the specified limit while preserving sensing accuracy, safety integrity, 
and intelligent monitoring functionality, making the system both economically feasible and 
technically credible.

Conclusions and Recommendations 
5.1 Conclusion 
The infrared-based methane leak detection system has been successfully designed, analyzed, 
and validated at a laboratory scale. The final design integrates near-infrared laser absorption 
sensing, analog signal conditioning, embedded digitization, AI-based classification, and cloud-
based monitoring into a unified multidisciplinary system. 
The Electrical Engineering analysis demonstrated that the laser excitation, optical detection, 
and signal conditioning subsystems operate within stable and measurable limits. The system 
achieves sufficient signal-to-noise ratio and maintains ADC resolution above the quantization 

--- PAGE 86 ---
 
 
 
87 
threshold required for reliable methane-induced attenuation detection. The sampling rate of 10 
Hz satisfies the specified response time requirement. 
From a Petroleum Engineering perspective, methane detection was evaluated relative to the 
Lower Explosive Limit (LEL), ensuring operation within a conservative sub-LEL regime. The 
test validation framework confirmed that the sensing configuration can detect localized joint-
level methane release before approaching hazardous concentrations, aligning technical 
performance with practical safety interpretation. 
From a Computer Science perspective, the AI-based anomaly detection model successfully 
distinguishes methane-induced absorption patterns from normal operating conditions. Feature 
extraction, supervised classification, and secure cloud communication collectively enhance 
detection reliability while reducing false alarms. 
The final design satisfies the defined technical specifications, including detection threshold, 
response time, secure communication, and operating constraints. The system demonstrates that 
an integrated infrared and AI-based methane monitoring solution can be implemented within 
the defined prototype cost and safety limitations. 
Overall, the project confirms the feasibility of combining optical sensing, embedded systems, 
and intelligent data analysis to provide a reliable early-warning methane leak detection platform 
suitable for further industrial development. 
 
5.2 Recommendations 
Based on the current prototype stage, several improvements are recommended for future 
development and industrial deployment. 
First, future work should focus on implementing a fully integrated optical module with 
improved mechanical alignment stability. A ruggedized enclosure and vibration-resistant 
mounting structure would enhance long-term field reliability under real pipeline conditions. 
Second, the system should be evaluated under outdoor environmental conditions, including 
varying temperature, humidity, and airflow, to validate performance beyond laboratory testing. 

--- PAGE 87 ---
 
 
 
88 
Incorporating environmental compensation algorithms may further improve detection 
robustness. 
Third, the AI classification model can be enhanced using real field-collected methane leak 
datasets instead of synthetic or laboratory-generated data. Expanding the training dataset would 
improve model generalization and detection accuracy in practical scenarios. 
Fourth, future designs may integrate a dedicated low-power embedded processing unit 
optimized for industrial deployment, reducing overall power consumption and enabling 
extended autonomous operation. 
Finally, a pilot-scale field trial in collaboration with an industry partner is recommended to 
evaluate the system’s scalability, communication reliability, and operational effectiveness in 
real pipeline environments. 
These enhancements would transition the current laboratory prototype toward a deployable 
industrial methane leak monitoring solution.
